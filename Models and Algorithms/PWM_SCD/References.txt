References
Lobry, J. R., & Chessel, D. (2003). Gene Prediction in Prokaryotes: A Historical Perspective. In Methods in Microbiology, vol. 30, 99–115. Elsevier.
This book chapter provides an overview of gene prediction in prokaryotes, covering the principles of ORF identification, start and stop codon recognition, and codon usage bias.

Salzberg, S. L. (1999). A Survey of Computational Methods in Microbial Genome Annotation. Annual Review of Genomics and Human Genetics, 2, 209–237.
This review discusses various computational approaches for microbial gene prediction, including the role of ribosome binding sites, start codons, and statistical models.

Borodovsky, M., & McIninch, J. (1993). GeneMark: Parallel Gene Recognition for Both DNA Strands. Computers & Chemistry, 17(2), 123–133.
GeneMark was one of the first tools to incorporate coding potential based on codon usage bias, making it foundational in the field of gene prediction in prokaryotes.

Kingsford, C., Delcher, A. L., & Salzberg, S. L. (2007). A Novel Compression Scheme for Biological Sequences and Its Applications. BMC Bioinformatics, 8(1), 159.
This paper introduces machine learning approaches for bioinformatics tasks, emphasizing the potential of neural networks and other algorithms for gene prediction.

Macke, T. J., & Green, P. J. (2005). Ribosome Binding Sites and Translation Initiation in Prokaryotes. Cell, 112(5), 737–745.
This article reviews the biological and computational aspects of identifying ribosome binding sites in prokaryotes and how these sites contribute to translation initiation.

Jones, D. T. (1999). Protein Secondary Structure Prediction Based on Position-Specific Scoring Matrices. Computers in Biology and Medicine, 29(3), 185–199.
While this paper is on protein structure prediction, it also discusses the application of position-specific scoring matrices (PSSMs) in sequence prediction models, a concept relevant to gene prediction in coding and non-coding regions.

Delcher, A. L., Bratke, K. A., Powers, E. C., & Salzberg, S. L. (2007). Identifying Prokaryotic Genes by Training on Known Genes. BMC Bioinformatics, 8(1), 37.
This research paper covers training gene prediction models on validated prokaryotic genes, emphasizing the importance of verified datasets for accurate gene identification.
